Forecasting the stock market in the time of Corona

Translations: en
Fr 13 MÃ¤rz 2020

On 21.01.2020 the World Health Organization (WHO) released its first official situation report on what they termed the Novel Coronavirus (2019-nCov). A then total of 282 confirmed cases in four countries (China, Japan, South Korea and Thailand) has by the time of this writing (the 51th WHO situation report on 11.3.2020) turned into over 100.000 confirmed cases with incidences in basically every country on earth. With commmunity-wide quarantine measurements taking place in many countries and an almost full time media coverage going on almost from day one, it was only a matter of time before the stock markets around the world started panicking as well.

I'm not an expert in disease control procedures, so it is not my purpose to give any kind of advice on that front, so why do I feel the need to say anything at all about it? Well, turns out that the crisis broke out exactly when I was getting familiar with forecasting methods for time series, so it seemed like a perfect opportunity to test my newly acquired skills on this dataset. This post tries to answer the question: how will the stock market evolve along with the health crisis that now goes by the name of Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19)? To be concrete, I will attempt to predict how the german stock index DAX30 - listing the major 30 companies trading on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange - will continue to evolve in the face of rising confirmed cases with some simple, easily understandable models.

Model identification¶

With cases worldwide piling up, the DAX30 index seemed to be stable and even reached the all time record high of 13789 in week 4 of the outbreak, two days after the massive spike of 20.000 cases (mostly in China) reported on 17.02.2020. One can infer from the plot that the number of confirmed cases in Germany did affect the index a lot more negatively than the total number of cases. This can be confirmed by looking at the correlation of the index with all cases

With this short preliminary analysis, we can try to predict the value of the index at a given time $\mathrm{dax}_t$ as a function of itself at previous times (as well as the No. of cases $\mathrm{covid}_t$). One way to see how far back previous values affect the current value is to examine the autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation functions.

From the right plot it seems only the value at lag 1 contributes significantly. In other words, it seems we can consider a functional dependence of the form $$ \mathrm{dax}_t= f\left( \mathrm{dax}_{t-1}, \mathrm{covid}_t\right) $$ but no values at previous times make a significant contribution. Because I'm aiming for simplicity, I would like to use a linear model for the function $f$, which has a good chance to work if $\mathrm{dax}_t$ is stationary. This is clearly not the case, but with a bit of trial and error we can see that its second difference is. This is confirmed by an augmented Dicky-Fuller test:

In [7]:
adf_dax=adfuller(dax_corona.diff().diff().dropna())
print('ADF Statistic for the second difference of DAX: %f, p-value %2.2e' % (adf_dax[0], adf_dax[1]))

ADF Statistic for the second difference of DAX: -10.818460, p-value 1.83e-19

Since the p-value is close to zero, we can expect the second difference to be stationary. From the partial autocorrelation plots we know only the values at lag 1 contribute significantly, so our prediction model will take the form of an autoregressive model of order 1 in the difference of order 2. This is known as an ARIMA(1,2,0)-process. The effect of $\mathrm{covid}_t$ (included as an external variable) turns our model into what is called an ARIMAX(1,2,0) process with exogenous variable $\mathrm{covid}_t$. This means the mathematical model for prediction we use is

$$\Delta^2\mathrm{dax}_t=\mu + \phi \Delta^2\mathrm{dax}_{t-1} + \eta \Delta^2\log(\mathrm{covid})_t + \epsilon_t$$

.

where $\epsilon_t$ is a noise term, and the appeareance of the logarithm of $\mathrm{covid}_t$ renders the whole expression stationary, as confirmed by the ADF test below. The drift $\mu$ and the parameters $\phi$ and $\eta$ are what we want to determine.

adf_covid=adfuller(np.log(df.Germany['2020-01-28':]).diff().diff().dropna())

We want to be able to make predictions beyond the available dataset, and for this we need to know the values of the external variable in the future. A crude, simple way to estimate the number of Covid cases is to assume a fixed growth rate $\alpha$ and model the one step ahead evolution as

$$\mathrm{covid}_{t+1}=\mathrm{covid}_t\left(1+\alpha\right).$$

The growth rate $\alpha$ itself can just be estimated as the average percentage of growth rate starting from the day of the first confirmed case. This is implemented below to obtain the estimate starting from the last number of confirmed cases (11.03.2020) and 5 days into the future

In [63]:
alpha=df.loc['2020-01-29':,'Germany'].pct_change().mean()
forecast_dates=pd.date_range(start='2020-03-11', end='2020-03-16', freq='D')

def forecast_cases(dates):
    n=dates.size
    #The first entry corresponds to the latest official number of confirmed cases (included for plotting purposes)!
    cases=[np.round(df.Germany.iloc[-1]*(1+alpha)**i) for i in range(n)]
    return cases

estimate=pd.DataFrame(forecast_cases(forecast_dates),index=forecast_dates,columns=['Estimated'])

Select and train the model¶

Since we have relatively few data points a train-test split in the usual way, i.e.

$$X_{train}=X_{t<\tau} \mbox{ and }X_{test}=X_{t\geq\tau}$$

would most likely over/underestimate the strong tendency of the last couple of days, namely that the DAX index drops rapidly while the number of COVID-19 cases grows exponentially. Given that this is precisely what we want to estimate we want to have as many training datapoints in the recent days. For this reason the train set consists of just all data points after the first confirmed case, and the test set will be (for now) one point: the official data of 12.03.2020.

If need be, this can be adjusted by reducing the number of training points while still retaining all the most recent events in the data, i.e. by increasing $\tau$ in

$$X_{train}=X_{t\geq\tau}$$
In [10]:
# Starting date for the training set as control parameter
date_split='2020-01-29'

# Training sets for endogenous and exogenous variables
exog=np.log(df.Germany[date_split:])
endog=dax_corona[date_split:]

Even though the prime candidate model is an ARIMA(1,2,0) model, it's still good practice to do a cross validation on the different orders of ARIMA$(p,d,q)$ to make sure our model actually minimizes goodness of fit in the form of AIC and/or BIC. Performing this we actually see that $d=2$ and $q=0$ are the ideal degrees but both AIC and BIC select $p=2$ instead of $p=1$. In other words, we can expect the model

$$\Delta^2\mathrm{dax}_t=\mu + \phi_1 \Delta^2\mathrm{dax}_{t-1} + \phi_2 \Delta^2\mathrm{dax}_{t-2} + \eta \Delta^2\log(\mathrm{covid})_t + \epsilon_t$$

to give the best forecasts. Below we will add some of the results of the forecast of our original guess - ARIMAX(1,2,0) for comparison.

Concluding remarks¶

A final check reveals that the approximations regarding the growth of cases are within 2.8% of the right values, with WHO status report number 52 (12.03.2020) claiming 1567 (vs my estimated 1524) confirmed COVID-19 cases in Germany. The full autoregressive model, however, fails to catch the steady drop of the DAX30 index, that closed at 9161,13 on that day, i.e. considerably below the lower end of the predicted confidence interval!

That probably doesn't mean it is the time to sell your shares and invest on toilet paper https://9gag.com/gag/aBgzQ71. Most likely, it just means that it is not just the virus outbreak what is driving the DAX index down, and that there is considerable room for improvement to the model by, e.g. considering some other exogenous variables (like oil prices) or including a training set that includes either similar index drops (like the red and green areas of the plot below, each of which were potentially strongly correlated to some sort of national crisis).
